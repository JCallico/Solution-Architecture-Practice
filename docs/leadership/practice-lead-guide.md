## üéØ Role Overview

### Position Summary

The Practice Lead (Director/Head of Solution Architecture) is responsible for:
- Leading the solution architecture practice across the organization
- Setting strategic direction for technology architecture
- Building and developing a high-performing architecture team
- Establishing governance, standards, and best practices
- Driving architectural excellence in all technology initiatives
- Influencing C-level technology strategy and decisions

### Key Stakeholders

| Stakeholder | Relationship | Engagement Frequency |
|------------|-------------|---------------------|
| CTO/CIO | Direct report, strategic partner | Weekly 1:1s |
| VP Engineering | Peer, collaboration partner | Bi-weekly |
| VP Product | Peer, alignment partner | Bi-weekly |
| Program Management | Service delivery coordination | Weekly |
| Security Leadership | Governance partner | Monthly |
| Engineering Managers | Team alignment | Monthly skip-levels |
| Architecture Team | Direct leadership | Daily/Weekly |

## üöÄ First 90 Days

### Days 1-30: Learn & Assess

**Week 1: Immersion**
- Meet with each member of the architecture team (1:1s)
- Review existing documentation, standards, and processes
- Attend ongoing architecture reviews and team meetings
- Schedule introductory meetings with key stakeholders
- Review current project portfolio and architecture involvement

**Week 2-3: Deep Dive**
- Conduct listening tour with engineering teams
- Review past architecture decisions and outcomes
- Assess current state of architecture practice maturity
- Identify quick wins and immediate improvements
- Understand budget, tools, and resource constraints

**Week 4: Synthesis**
- Document current state assessment (strengths, gaps, opportunities)
- Draft initial 90-day priorities
- Present findings to CTO and gather feedback
- Begin building relationships with executive stakeholders

### Days 31-60: Plan & Build Alignment

**Strategic Planning**
- Develop 12-month practice roadmap
- Define practice vision and success metrics
- Identify key initiatives and resource needs
- Build business case for any needed investments

**Team Development**
- Assess team skills and capabilities
- Identify hiring needs or gaps
- Begin career development conversations
- Establish team operating norms and rituals

**Stakeholder Alignment**
- Present practice vision to leadership team
- Align on governance approach and decision rights
- Establish regular touchpoints with key stakeholders
- Begin building architecture community of practice

### Days 61-90: Execute & Deliver

**Quick Wins**
- Implement 2-3 high-impact improvements
- Launch or refresh key processes (ARB, standards, etc.)
- Deliver value on 1-2 strategic initiatives
- Demonstrate practice impact through metrics

**Foundation Setting**
- Finalize annual plan and secure approval
- Complete team structure and hiring plan
- Establish governance framework
- Launch communication and visibility initiatives

### 1. Practice Strategy & Vision

**Define the "Why"**
- Articulate compelling vision for architecture practice
- Align practice mission with business objectives
- Communicate value proposition to stakeholders
- Set multi-year strategic direction

**Strategic Planning**
- Annual practice planning and roadmap
- Technology strategy input and influence
- Innovation agenda and emerging tech evaluation
- Make vs. buy vs. partner recommendations

**Example Deliverables:**
- Annual practice plan and OKRs
- Technology strategy recommendations
- Architecture maturity roadmap
- Innovation portfolio

### 2. Team Leadership & Development

**Build the Team**
- Define team structure and roles
- Recruit and hire top architecture talent
- Onboard new team members effectively
- Build diverse, inclusive team culture

**Develop People**
- Create career development framework
- Provide coaching and mentorship
- Facilitate learning and skill development
- Conduct performance reviews and promotions
- Manage compensation and rewards

**Foster Culture**
- Establish team values and operating principles
- Create psychological safety and trust
- Encourage collaboration and knowledge sharing
- Celebrate successes and learn from failures

**Key Activities:**
- Weekly 1:1s with direct reports
- Monthly team meetings and retrospectives
- Quarterly talent reviews
- Annual compensation planning

### 3. Governance & Standards

**Architecture Governance**
- Establish and lead Architecture Review Board
- Define architectural standards and principles
- Create exception and waiver process
- Monitor compliance and address violations

**Quality Assurance**
- Review critical architectural decisions
- Ensure consistency across initiatives
- Audit architecture documentation
- Drive continuous improvement

**Risk Management**
- Identify and escalate architectural risks
- Ensure proper risk mitigation strategies
- Review technical debt portfolio
- Balance innovation with stability

### 4. Organizational Influence

**Executive Partnership**
- Present to C-suite and board
- Influence technology investment decisions
- Shape organizational technology strategy
- Build credibility and trust with leadership

**Cross-Functional Collaboration**
- Partner with Product on roadmap and prioritization
- Align with Engineering on technical direction
- Collaborate with Security on risk management
- Work with PMO on delivery excellence

**Change Leadership**
- Drive architectural transformations
- Communicate change effectively
- Build consensus and overcome resistance
- Ensure successful adoption

### 5. Practice Operations

**Resource Management**
- Allocate architects to projects
- Balance demand vs. capacity
- Manage practice budget
- Optimize tooling and infrastructure

**Process Excellence**
- Define and optimize architecture processes
- Create templates and accelerators
- Measure process effectiveness
- Continuously improve workflows

**Knowledge Management**
- Build and maintain architecture knowledge base
- Document reference architectures and patterns
- Share lessons learned and best practices
- Create reusable assets

## üéØ Strategic Activities

### Annual Planning

**Q4 Planning Process (Oct-Dec)**

1. **Assess Current State**
   - Review practice performance vs. goals
   - Gather feedback from stakeholders
   - Analyze team capacity and utilization
   - Evaluate tool and process effectiveness

2. **Strategic Analysis**
   - Technology trends and emerging technologies
   - Competitive landscape and industry benchmarks
   - Business strategy and growth plans
   - Regulatory and compliance changes

3. **Set Direction**
   - Define next year's vision and themes
   - Establish practice OKRs
   - Prioritize initiatives and investments
   - Align with organizational goals

4. **Resource Planning**
   - Team size and hiring plan
   - Budget requirements
   - Tool and platform needs
   - Training and development investments

5. **Socialize & Align**
   - Present plan to leadership
   - Gather feedback and iterate
   - Secure budget approval
   - Communicate to team and stakeholders

### Quarterly Business Reviews

**Standing Agenda**
- Practice metrics and KPI review
- Key accomplishments and impact
- Team updates (hires, promotions, changes)
- Architecture governance outcomes
- Major architectural decisions and ADRs
- Risk and issue escalations
- Upcoming priorities and initiatives
- Stakeholder feedback and satisfaction

**Audience:** CTO, VP Engineering, VP Product, key stakeholders

### Technology Radar / Innovation Pipeline

**Quarterly Technology Assessment**
- Emerging technologies to adopt, trial, assess, or hold
- Innovation experiments and pilots
- Technical debt reduction initiatives
- Platform modernization roadmap

**Categories:**
- **Adopt** - Ready for production use, recommended
- **Trial** - Proven in pilots, use in non-critical projects
- **Assess** - Worth exploring, run experiments
- **Hold** - Proceed with caution or avoid

## üöß Common Challenges

### Challenge 1: "Architecture is a Bottleneck"

**Symptoms:**
- Engineering teams complain about slow approvals
- ARB backlog growing
- Projects starting without architecture review

**Solutions:**
- Implement tiered review process (light vs. deep reviews)
- Empower embedded architects with decision authority
- Create self-service templates and patterns
- Add capacity (hire or redistribute work)
- Automate compliance checking where possible
- Measure and optimize ARB cycle time

### Challenge 2: "Lack of Executive Buy-In"

**Symptoms:**
- Architecture recommendations ignored
- Budget cuts or headcount freezes
- Not invited to strategic discussions
- Decisions made without architectural input

**Solutions:**
- Speak in business value terms, not technical jargon
- Quantify impact (cost, speed, risk reduction)
- Build relationships through regular 1:1s
- Demonstrate quick wins and credibility
- Align architecture initiatives with business priorities
- Create executive-friendly metrics and dashboards

### Challenge 3: "Ivory Tower Perception"

**Symptoms:**
- Engineering teams bypass architecture
- Architects seen as blockers, not enablers
- Low adoption of standards and patterns
- "Not invented here" resistance

**Solutions:**
- Embed architects in delivery teams
- Make architects accountable for delivery outcomes
- Involve engineers in standards development
- Create office hours for consultation
- Celebrate teams following architecture guidance
- Show flexibility and pragmatism, not dogma

### Challenge 4: "Team Skills Gaps"

**Symptoms:**
- Architects lack depth in key areas
- Inconsistent quality of architecture work
- Unable to support new technologies
- Team struggling with modern practices

**Solutions:**
- Conduct skills assessment and gap analysis
- Create targeted learning plans
- Bring in external expertise (training, consulting)
- Hire for critical skill gaps
- Implement mentorship and peer learning
- Allow time for experimentation and learning

### Challenge 5: "Resistance to Standards"

**Symptoms:**
- Teams ignoring architecture standards
- Many exception requests
- Inconsistent technology choices
- Integration challenges

**Solutions:**
- Involve teams in creating standards
- Provide clear rationale and trade-offs
- Make compliance easy with templates and tools
- Show benefits through case studies
- Implement lightweight approval for low-risk exceptions
- Balance standardization with innovation
